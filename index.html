Activation Maximization through Gradient Ascent

This technique can be used to identify what a neural network is trained to see, which can help to inform an engineer whether or not the neural network was trained to percieve the correct patterns in an image. In this example, I've repurposed this method to randomly generate an image from an inverted Convolutional Neural Network, by choosing the Neural Network layers that interest me and feeding an RGB image of random noise into it. The program adjusts the pixel values with gradient ascent, such that the output of the layers will be maximized. Pretty awesome right?!

What is Cognitive Science, Machine Learning and Neural Computation?

My experience exploring this major at UC San Diego includes a study of cognitive systems, neural networks, calculus, linear algebra, statistics, and computer science. By wielding these scientific foundations, we can build Reinforcement Learning Agents, Computer Vision applications, and other highly complex deep neural networks that will self-driving cars, Netflix recommendations, voice assistants, and all the cool tech-y stuff that most people take for granted.

Building an innovative product is easier than ever with this rising techonology. If you have relevant data, chances are, you can dream pretty big in the field of Machine Learning.

Want to detect wildfires automatically by using cameras and previous videos of wildfires? You can use a detection and/or segmentation model that learns from patterns in the images of previous fires to identify where on the image smoke is pluming, before a human can see it. Neat, right? It would be nice to have less wildfires in California, or even none. At my current internship at the San Diego Supercomputer Center, we are building a solution to this very problem by developing Deep Neural Networks in PyTorch, with the intent to deploy them on edge devices at high altitude weather stations.

Take another problem with less predictability and no past examples, like identifying every type of cell that exists in the brain of a mammal for the first time. Neuroscientists at UC San Diego are collecting the brain samples of rodents and using unsupervised learning techniques like t-SNE, UMAP, and hierarchical clustering to find patterns in the RNA-seq, methylation and chromatin accessibility to develop the first taxonomy of a mammalian brain. I was lucky to be a part of a small team of 2 other software engineers to build the data pipelines and visualization platform for the principal researchers to interact with.

This is my personal experience with my major in Cognitive Science and Machine Learning at UCSD. I haven't graduated yet; I'll finish my undergraduate degree in June 2021.

Internship: Detecting Wildfires using Mask R-CNN Pt.2

This is much better. The next step is to try an LSTM approach where our model can remember predictions of previous frames, instead of simply searching for smoke frame-by frame. To do so, I will start by developing a Faster R-CNN that will feed the outputs to an LSTM. The Faster R-CNN will perform the detection and create bounding boxes that outline the image we want to feed to the LSTM. The intuition is that feeding a sequence of images will reduce the false positives of our system and increase the precision by monitoring the development of the region of interest over time by including the relevant information of past predictions.

Internship: Detecting Wildfires using Mask R-CNN Pt.1

Its a start. You see that the smoke is consistently predicted when it is there, but there are obvious false positives: clouds. Another issue is that non-maximum supression (NMS) is not removing other predictions on the same smoke because the Intersection over Union (IoU) is smaller than the NMS threshold. I either have to adjust this threshold or handle Mask R-CNN outputs with post-processing to favor the largest of the overlapping bounding box and segmentations.
